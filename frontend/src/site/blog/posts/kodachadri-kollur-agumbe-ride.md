Some weekends you just need to get on the bike and ride. This was one of those.

![My Yezdi Adventure loaded up, with the Malnad hills rolling out behind](/blog/kodachadri/bike-malnad.jpg)

*Loaded up and pointed at the hills.*

## Shimoga → Nagara → Kodachadri

I set off from Shimoga and made my first stop at **Nagara Fort** — quiet, green, and a good
excuse to stretch my legs before the real adventure began.

![Standing with the bike on a tree-canopied Malnad road](/blog/kodachadri/on-the-road.jpg)

*The kind of road that makes you slow down on purpose.*

Then came **Kodachadri**. You can't take your own vehicle to the top, so the last stretch is an
open **jeep hill-climb** — and honestly, that ride was half the fun. Bouncing up the rough trail,
hanging on for dear life, everyone laughing at every jolt. So much fun.

At the top I met a few people from **Udupi, Beltangady and Hebri**. Total strangers, but so warm
and friendly — we ended up spending a good while together up there, just talking.

The summit was wrapped in thick **fog** — no view, zero visibility. And somehow that didn't matter
at all. Standing inside a cloud, cold wind cutting through, laughter all around — it was one of
those experiences that's better felt than photographed.

![On the fog-covered summit of Kodachadri in a raincoat](/blog/kodachadri/kodachadri-fog.jpg)

*The top of Kodachadri — no view, all vibe.*

Near the peak is the spot where **Adi Shankaracharya** is said to have meditated. As the legend
goes, it was here that Goddess Mookambika appeared to him, and he went on to consecrate her at
Kollur, at the foot of the hill. A small stone mantapa — the Sarvajna Peetha — marks where he sat.
Standing there in the fog and silence, you can sort of understand why he chose this place.

What really got me, though, were the **80–90 year old grandmas and grandpas** climbing those
hills. No fuss, no complaints — just quiet determination. Humbling.

## The ghat down to Kollur

From there we rode down to **Kollur**. The ghat road in between was **slippery** in patches — you
had to respect it — but winding through those green folds of hill was gorgeous.

![Standing on a fallen log over a forest stream near a small waterfall](/blog/kodachadri/stream-stop.jpg)

*The Ghats are full of little stops like this one.*

We stayed the night in Kollur and went for **darshana** at the Mookambika temple. Being a weekend,
it was packed — and from what I could tell, most of the crowd had come from **Kerala**. Even with
the numbers, the darshanam was smooth and calm.

## The loop back — Keradi, Moodugallu, Agumbe

Next morning we pointed the bikes towards **Agumbe**, taking the scenic long way.

![The Yezdi parked on a bridge over a Malnad river](/blog/kodachadri/river-bridge.jpg)

*The Yezdi earning its "Adventure" badge.*

First stop: **Keradi**, one of the places where **Kantara** was shot. There's a small thrill in
standing where a film you loved came to life.

Then **Moodugallu**, and the **Keshavanath cave temple** (Shiva). You actually walk *into* the
cave, through water, to reach the deity — and the stream inside is full of **fish**. People were
feeding them; a strangely peaceful little ritual in the dark.

![Entrance to the water-filled Keshavanath cave temple at Moodugallu](/blog/kodachadri/keshavanath-cave.jpg)

*Into the Keshavanath cave temple — you wade through the water to reach the deity.*

After that, the classics: the **Agumbe sunset viewpoint**, and then **Kundadri**, which has one of
the nicest viewpoints of the entire trip.

![Two bonnet macaques sitting on a mossy viewpoint railing](/blog/kodachadri/agumbe-monkeys.jpg)

*The locals at the viewpoint, entirely unbothered.*

![Panoramic view of green valleys and distant hills from Kundadri](/blog/kodachadri/kundadri-view.jpg)

*The view from Kundadri — worth every hairpin.*

We stopped for **lunch at Tirthahalli** and rolled back into **Shimoga by 7pm** — tired, a little
sunburnt, and completely happy.

## What stayed with me

Not the views — there weren't many, thanks to the fog. It was the people. The strangers on the
hilltop, the grandparents out-climbing all of us, the quiet feeding of fish in a cave. Sometimes
the best rides aren't about the destination at all.
